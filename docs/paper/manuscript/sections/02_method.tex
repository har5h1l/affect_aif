\section{Model and Experiments}

We evaluate partner-specific affective precision in a repeated trust game derived from the economic trust-game tradition \cite{Berg1995Trust,KingCasas2005Trust}. A focal active-inference agent interacts with four partners. Each partner has a latent type (cooperator, reciprocator, exploiter, or random) and a latent stance toward the agent (trusting, neutral, or hostile). The agent observes the partner's action and its own payoff, and it selects either a binary action or a graded investment depending on the regime.

In the binary game, both players choose cooperate or defect. Mutual cooperation gives the focal agent payoff $3$, unilateral cooperation against a defecting partner gives $-1$, unilateral defection against a cooperating partner gives $5$, and mutual defection gives $1$. Graded regimes replace the binary cooperate/defect choice with a discretized investment level, but preserve the same social problem: the agent must decide how much to rely on a partner whose type and stance are hidden. Partner choice regimes add one more decision: before acting, the focal agent chooses which partner to engage.

For each partner $k$, the focal agent maintains a separate discrete active-inference model over partner type, partner stance, and own-action bookkeeping:
\begin{equation}
s_{t,k} = \left(s^{\mathrm{type}}_{t,k}, s^{\mathrm{stance}}_{t,k}, s^{\mathrm{own}}_{t}\right).
\end{equation}
The partner model is a discrete generative model
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{M}_k = \{A^{\mathrm{action}}_k,A^{\mathrm{payoff}}_k,B_k,C_k,D_k,E_k\},
\end{equation}
where $A^{\mathrm{action}}_k$ maps hidden type and stance to the partner's response, $A^{\mathrm{payoff}}_k$ maps own action and partner response to the payoff observation, $B_k$ encodes state transitions, $D_k$ stores prior beliefs, $C_k$ encodes preferred observations, and $E_k$ stores policy priors. Payoff is therefore represented as an observation modality inside the generative model rather than as an external reward cache. State and policy inference use the standard discrete active-inference machinery implemented in \texttt{pymdp} \cite{Friston2017ProcessTheory,DaCosta2020DiscreteSynthesis,Heins2022Pymdp}. The trust-task code constructs the matrices, manages the partner bank, applies the external beta tracker, and records the behavioral and inferential measurements; it is not a custom replacement for the active-inference engine.

Partner choice is handled by evaluating partner-local policies and selecting among the resulting candidate interactions. In assigned-partner regimes, the agent chooses only the trust-game action. In partner-choice regimes, it first chooses whom to engage and then chooses an action toward that partner. This separates local social inference from the higher-level question of approach, avoidance, or probing: a partner can be predictable enough to avoid confidently, uncertain enough to probe, or reliable enough to return to.

The affective component is external to the POMDP state space. After interacting with partner $k$, the agent computes partner-action surprise
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_k = 1 - P(o^{\mathrm{action}}_k = o^{\mathrm{obs}}_k \mid q(s_{\mathrm{type}},s_{\mathrm{stance}})).
\end{equation}
This is a bounded prediction-error proxy in $[0,1]$, not logarithmic surprisal. It is computed from partner action rather than payoff because partner action is the most direct measurement of type-by-stance predictive fit.

The tracker converts this into affective charge
\begin{equation}
\phi_k = \alpha(\sigma_0^2 - \epsilon_k^2),
\end{equation}
following the interpretation of affect as expected action precision and model fitness \cite{Hesp2021DeeplyFeltAffect}. Positive charge means the partner model predicted better than the baseline variance $\sigma_0^2$; negative charge means it predicted worse. A categorical beta posterior $q(\beta_k)$ is updated over discrete beta levels $\{0.5,0.67,1.0,1.5,2.0\}$. The expected beta then modulates policy precision for future choices involving that partner:
\begin{equation}
\gamma_k = \gamma_{\mathrm{base}} / \mathbb{E}_{q(\beta_k)}[\beta_k].
\end{equation}
Lower beta therefore sharpens the policy posterior; higher beta softens it. A tracked-only lesion keeps the beta update but fixes $\gamma_k=\gamma_{\mathrm{base}}$, isolating deployment from belief updating.

The experiments use several behavioral and inferential measurements rather than a single payoff score. Policy entropy measures whether the action posterior is open enough for precision to matter. Correlations with surprise and payoff test what the beta-derived precision signal tracks. Lesion comparisons separate belief accuracy from policy deployment, partner-choice regimes measure approach and avoidance, and betrayal regimes test whether the same mechanism remains useful when a partner changes abruptly. Perturbation variants are treated as supplemental checks on beta dynamics rather than clinical validation.
