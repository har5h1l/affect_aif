\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

The simulations are organized around three empirical questions about relationship-specific confidence. First, partner-local affective precision should track predictive reliability rather than realized value, and this signal should be weakened when evidence is pooled across partners. Second, its behavioral effects should arise through action sharpening rather than improved partner-state beliefs, with consequences for both policy entropy and partner engagement. Third, under social change, precision dynamics should reveal temporal boundary conditions and parameterized profiles of trust revision. The subsections below evaluate these questions using reliability probes, shared-$\beta$ ablations, deployment controls, partner-choice regimes, abrupt betrayal, and profile-style perturbations.

\subsection{Precision tracks predictability, not partner value}
\label{sec:model_fitness}

The affective charge formula uses action surprisal rather than payoff, so $\beta_k$ is sensitive to behavioral consistency rather than realized value. Once the agent has inferred a partner's type and stance, a reliably defecting partner can generate low surprisal despite poor payoff, while an erratic partner can generate high surprisal despite occasionally favorable outcomes. The precision signal is therefore expected to track the quality of the agent's predictive model, not the desirability of the partner's behavior.

The 30-seed model-fitness run produced a clear reliability--value dissociation. Across partner-seed units, partner-local $\beta_k$ shows a stronger absolute association between precision and action surprisal than between precision and realized payoff ($0.949$ versus $0.748$). Because payoff, surprise, and exposure are correlated in partner-choice episodes, the stricter active-encounter partial readout is the more important test: after controlling encounter imbalance, the partner-local precision--surprise association remains strong ($0.882$), whereas the precision--payoff association falls to $0.130$. A single shared $\beta$ tracker preserves the ordering but with weaker relational specificity (partial associations $0.380$ versus $0.049$). Aggregate payoff moved in the opposite direction (partner-local: $483.5$; shared: $517.6$; no-affect: $542.1$), separating the precision signal from reward maximization. Partner-local precision therefore tracked predictive model fitness more cleanly than realized partner value. Figure~\ref{fig:model_fitness_dissociation} visualizes this dissociation for partner-local versus shared precision tracking.

\subsection{Shared precision tests relational specificity}
\label{sec:shared_beta}

The locality probe above tests whether confidence remains tied to the partner whose evidence produced it. Pooling evidence across partners weakens this relational specificity: a shared $\beta$ tracker preserves the coarse ordering between predictive surprise and realized payoff, but attenuates the partner-specific dissociation observed under partner-local precision. In the active-encounter partial readout, partner-local precision shows a stronger association with surprise than payoff, whereas shared precision compresses both associations toward weaker values.

The shared-$\beta$ comparison is a mechanistic locality test, not a definitive behavioral superiority test. Pooling evidence blurred the model-fitness signal across relationships. A separate mixed-volatility comparison would be needed to test whether this attenuation also changes partner allocation. The mixed-volatility analyses below provide a related boundary condition: stronger precision dynamics can increase payoff while also increasing false-positive confidence reduction toward stable partners. Thus, relationship-specific $\beta_k$ preserved relational specificity in the affective precision signal, while behavioral consequences under mixed volatility remain a target for future tests.

\subsection{Behavioral effects arise through action sharpening}
\label{sec:deployment}

A tracked-only ablation updates $q(\beta_k)$ normally but fixes $\gamma_k = \gamma_{\mathrm{base}}$, allowing the precision estimate to be analyzed without letting it affect action selection. In the open graded decision regime, full partner-local precision lowers policy entropy relative to both baselines ($8.6$ versus $8.8$ for no-affect and tracked-only), while total payoff is flat-to-slightly lower ($1851.3$ versus $1864.2$ for both baselines). Because tracked-only remains near the no-affect baseline while full precision modulation differs from both on entropy, the behavioral effect is localized to the $\beta_k \rightarrow \gamma_k$ deployment pathway rather than improved partner-state inference. Figure~\ref{fig:deployment_dissociation} summarizes the three-way contrast across affect, no-affect, and tracked-only variants.

\subsection{Partner engagement reorganizes around reliability}
\label{sec:partner_choice}

In partner-choice regimes the precision signal shapes not only how the agent behaves toward a chosen partner but which partners it selects in the first place. Policy entropy under precision modulation is $4.66$ versus $4.81$ without it, and engagement shows a modest redistribution across partner types. The partner-local agent selected the cooperator on $36.6\%$ of rounds and the reciprocator on $31.6\%$, while allocating $13.8\%$ and $18.0\%$ of choices to the exploiter and random partners, respectively. The no-affect control selected the same partners at $34.8\%$, $31.8\%$, $16.2\%$, and $17.2\%$, respectively. Aggregate payoff is flat at this scale ($377.3$ versus $385.3$), consistent with selection reorganization occurring before cumulative payoff differences have time to accumulate.

These allocation readouts show modest social reorganization without a reliable payoff separation at this replication scale.

\subsection{Abrupt betrayal reveals the mechanism's temporal structure}
\label{sec:betrayal}

When a previously cooperative partner switches stance mid-episode, the agent enters the post-switch period with $\beta_k$ reflecting a well-validated model that no longer applies. In the 30-seed abrupt-betrayal analysis, partner-local affective precision produces a small payoff advantage relative to the no-affect control ($1185.9$ versus $1172.1$), but the paired bootstrap interval for the difference crosses zero ($-25.2$ to $53.2$). The robust effect is instead the deployment signature: policy entropy is lower under precision modulation ($8.36$ versus $8.74$; bootstrap interval for the difference $-0.62$ to $-0.14$), confirming that the action-confidence pathway is active.

Joint partner-type accuracy is higher under precision modulation ($0.372$ versus $0.266$; bootstrap interval for the difference $0.034$ to $0.185$). Under abrupt social change, partner-local precision produced a robust entropy effect, higher joint partner-type accuracy, and an uncertain payoff advantage. The result highlights a temporal dependency: accumulated confidence helps only when portfolio structure and reallocation speed allow the agent to redirect commitment after change.

This temporal dependence motivates the parameter analyses below: if accumulated confidence can help or hurt depending on the speed and structure of change, then precision gain and prior model fitness should define distinct profiles of social trust revision.

\subsection{Precision dynamics as computational profiles of social trust calibration}
\label{sec:phenotypes}

The parameters governing $\beta_k$ dynamics define a controlled profile program. Variation in precision gain $\alpha$ and the prior over model fitness produces qualitatively distinct behavioral profiles across social contexts. These profiles provide computational analogues for empirically studied variation in social trust calibration \cite{Behrens2008SocialLearning,Adams2013ComputationalPsychosis,Cittern2018AttachmentActiveInference}. They are not clinical classifications or fitted human profiles.

Profile-inspired computational perturbations (Appendix~\ref{app:extended_results}) show that flattened precision dynamics can collapse action commitment without improving payoff: the low-gain variant compresses $\beta_k$ range to $0.24$ and raises entropy to $8.9$, near the no-affect and tracked-only baselines, whereas expanded-dynamics variants widen $\beta_k$ movement without monotonic payoff gains. This motivates parameter sweeps over gain and prior structure rather than a single high-versus-low trust axis.

\paragraph{Precision-gain axis.}
Sweeping $\alpha$ across $\{0.05, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 4.0, 8.0\}$ shows that $\beta_k$ movement range increases monotonically with $\alpha$ in the betrayal regime. Low-$\alpha$ agents ($\alpha \le 0.1$) show near-zero exploitation rates and Gini coefficients of $0.423$ and $0.397$, producing diffuse, slowly-updating engagement patterns. High-$\alpha$ agents ($\alpha \ge 4.0$) show Gini coefficients of $0.366$ and $0.408$ with recovery times of $28.8$ and $13.0$ rounds respectively---faster detection of behavioral change in some runs, but higher variance in recovery outcomes. Payoff does not rank $\alpha$ values monotonically across either regime (betrayal: range $1914.6$--$1986.2$; open graded: $1906.3$--$1987.7$), indicating that the behavioral consequence of precision-gain depends on task context rather than gain magnitude alone. Extended alpha-sweep diagnostics are in Appendix~\ref{app:extended_results}.

\paragraph{Profile quadrants.}
Crossing prior belief (naive versus cautious) with $\alpha$ (low versus high) defines four behavioral profiles tested in the betrayal environment (20 seeds). Low-$\alpha$ profiles (naive-stubborn and avoidant-rigid) show substantially smaller $\beta_k$ range ($0.32$--$0.36$) than high-$\alpha$ profiles ($0.89$--$1.08$), confirming that gain controls the magnitude of precision dynamics. Naive-stubborn shows the lowest Gini ($0.304$), reflecting maximally diffuse partner selection consistent with a slowly-updating confidence profile. Avoidant-rigid shows the lowest payoff ($1889$), as the cautious prior prevents confidence from building even toward reliably cooperative partners. The anxious-reactive profile shows the highest post-betrayal commitment errors toward the switched partner (post-betrayal P0 selection: $0.319$; high investment rate: $0.121$), reflecting rapid confidence accumulation that becomes a liability when a partner's behavior changes. The default agent outperforms all profile variants on payoff ($1991$), occupying a calibrated region of the gain-prior space. Quadrant figures and supporting diagnostics are in Appendix~\ref{app:extended_results}.

\paragraph{Forgiveness and trust repair.}
In the forgiveness paradigm, the betrayed partner reverts to cooperative/trusting behavior after round 120. The main pattern separates reengagement from restored confidence. No-affect and cautious-low-$\alpha$ agents reengage most often after repair (selection rates $0.593$ and $0.630$), while default and naive-high-$\alpha$ agents reengage less ($0.475$ and $0.560$). Payoff recovery is near baseline across all conditions ($0.996$--$1.033$), separating reengagement from payoff recovery rather than producing a simple ``more affect means better forgiveness'' pattern. Low gain produces muted $\beta_k$ dynamics and rapid reapproach in this task (cautious-low latency $1.5$ rounds; naive-low $4.6$), whereas high-gain profiles show larger confidence swings but not a higher payoff-recovery ceiling. Trust repair is therefore a profile-dependent dissociation: willingness to sample the reverted partner and restoration of model-fitness confidence can move separately.

\paragraph{Mixed-volatility discrimination.}
\label{sec:phenotypes_mixed}
The mixed-volatility environment places four partners with heterogeneous volatility in the same episode: a stationary cooperator (P0), a stationary exploiter (P1), an abrupt-switch partner (P2), and a gradual-drift partner (P3). Under partner choice, the agent must maintain stable engagement with P0 while updating away from P2 and P3 after their behavioral changes.

\midrule

\bottomrule

The false-positive rate---unwarranted confidence reduction toward the stationary P0---increases with $\alpha$: high-$\alpha$ ($0.484$), default ($0.422$), low-$\alpha$ ($0.359$), no-affect ($0.273$). The discrimination index does not simply improve with gain: low-$\alpha$ gives the strongest discrimination score ($0.213$), while high-$\alpha$ is negative ($-0.089$) despite achieving the highest payoff. Figure~\ref{fig:mixed_volatility} shows that payoff did not increase monotonically with gain. In this environment, high gain amplified reactivity and false positives toward stable partners, while payoff depended on how that reactivity interacted with partner allocation.

Higher precision-gain $\alpha$ can raise payoff while worsening discrimination and false-positive suppression toward P0; profiles are computational analogues, not clinical classifications.}

Together, the profile experiments show a family of confidence-revision profiles determined by gain and prior initial conditions. Across environments, confidence dynamics, partner allocation, and payoff dissociated rather than following a monotonic payoff ranking. Extended profile tables, alpha-sweep diagnostics, and forgiveness/trust-repair analyses are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:extended_results}.
