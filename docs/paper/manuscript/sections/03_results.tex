\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We report five sets of analyses. Section~\ref{sec:h1} establishes what the affective precision channel actually tracks. Section~\ref{sec:h2} shows that its behavioral effect depends on how open the policy space is, and that the effect runs through action sharpening rather than better partner beliefs. Section~\ref{sec:choice} examines partner selection. Sections~\ref{sec:betrayal} and~\ref{sec:shock} characterize the mechanism's volatility limit. Section~\ref{sec:pheno} reports precision-dynamics phenotypes as a parameter-sensitivity check.

\subsection{The affective precision channel tracks partner predictability, not partner value}
\label{sec:h1}

The central question for the affective precision channel is whether its running estimate of model fitness actually reflects predictive reliability or whether it is a proxy for experienced reward. A predictable exploiter is low value but easy to model; a valuable but erratic partner is worth engaging but hard to predict. If the channel tracks value, these two situations should produce similar precision levels. If it tracks model fitness, they should diverge.

We evaluate this in the binary game across 30 seeds and 200 rounds per seed. The affective precision channel ($\gamma_k$-derived signal) correlates more strongly with partner predictability than with payoff: $|\text{corr}(\text{precision}, \text{surprise})| = 0.701$ versus $|\text{corr}(\text{precision}, \text{payoff})| = 0.419$ across partner-seed units. The seed-level gap between these two correlations is $+0.096$, bootstrap CI $[0.027,\; 0.164]$. The channel is carrying partner-model reliability information, not reward history.

The binary regime also shows that having this reliability information does not automatically improve payoff: precision modulation yields $534.6$ versus $542.1$ for no-affect, mean difference $-7.5$, CI $[-27.9,\; 13.8]$. This is the expected pattern. In the binary game the expected free energy gap between cooperating and defecting is large enough that the policy posterior is already near-deterministic; sharpening or softening a near-argmax changes almost nothing. The channel is informative about reliability but has nowhere to act. Section~\ref{sec:h2} shows what happens when it does.

\subsection{Behavioral effects depend on policy entropy; action sharpening accounts for the benefit}
\label{sec:h2}

Whether the affective precision channel can improve behavior depends on how much room the policy space gives it to act. We compare five seeds per variant, 200 rounds, across both regimes. In the binary game: no payoff advantage, no entropy difference (Section~\ref{sec:h1}). In the graded investment game: precision modulation reaches total payoff $1884.6$ versus $1859.4$ for the no-affect baseline ($+25.2$), with mean policy entropy $7.94$ versus $8.80$ ($-0.86$). The improvement emerges precisely in the regime where policy entropy is high enough for action confidence to matter.

The tracked-only lesion establishes that this benefit comes from action sharpening, not from better partner beliefs. In the graded regime, the tracked-only agent---which updates $\beta_k$ normally but never uses it to set $\gamma_k$---has joint type-disposition belief accuracy $0.339$ and stance accuracy $0.855$, slightly better than the precision-modulated agent's $0.336$ and $0.816$. Its beliefs are at least as good, yet its payoff and entropy track the no-affect baseline rather than the precision-modulated agent. The behavioral gain is attributable specifically to the $\beta_k \to \gamma_k$ pathway. The affective precision channel augments social inference not by improving partner-model accuracy---something like better ToM---but by regulating how confidently those models are acted upon.

\subsection{Partner selection reorganizes around reliability}
\label{sec:choice}

In partner-choice regimes the agent's action-confidence estimates shape not just how it behaves toward a chosen partner, but which partners it chooses to engage in the first place. Aggregate payoff is a blunt measure here; subtle shifts in selection patterns can reflect meaningful reorientation before payoff has had time to accumulate.

Across five seeds and 200 rounds, aggregate payoff is nearly flat: $393.6$ versus $393.2$. But policy entropy under precision modulation is $3.99$ versus $4.83$ without it, and selection rates shift in the expected direction: the most predictable partner draws more engagement ($0.348 \to 0.366$), a less predictable partner draws less ($0.162 \to 0.138$). The affective precision channel is reorganizing social engagement around partner-model reliability---concentrating interaction where the agent can act confidently---at a scale visible in selection behavior before it registers in payoff.

\subsection{Abrupt betrayal is the mechanism's volatility limit}
\label{sec:betrayal}

All conditions above involve stationary partners. Betrayal scenarios test what happens when a partner's disposition changes mid-episode: the agent enters the post-switch period with a model that has been accumulating positive affective charge under the old behavioral regime.

The problem is precisely that the affective precision channel has been doing its job. The agent has built up action confidence toward the betraying partner based on a model that no longer applies. When the partner changes, the $\beta_k$ posterior still reflects the pre-switch reliability record while the lower-level partner-state beliefs---which need repeated evidence to reorganize---are only beginning to update. The channel sharpens action based on accumulated confidence in a stale model.

Across 30 seeds and 120 rounds this is what we observe. Precision modulation yields lower whole-run payoff than the no-affect and tracked-only baselines: $1136.1$ versus $1172.1$, mean difference $-36.1$, bootstrap CI $[-63.7,\; -10.9]$, $p = 0.017$. Policy entropy is lower under precision modulation ($8.38$ vs.\ $8.74$), confirming the channel is active and influencing action. Post-switch, the agent returns to the switched partner less often ($4.4$ vs.\ $6.1$ reencounters; selection rate $0.049$ vs.\ $0.067$) but with no calibration improvement: payoff per reencounter $8.76$ vs.\ $8.91$, wrong-type rate $0.237$ vs.\ $0.165$. The agent acted decisively; its inference did not justify that decisiveness.

\subsection{The limit is the speed of change, not the amount of precision}
\label{sec:shock}

The betrayal failure might suggest the problem is too much precision gain---a $\beta_k$ update that is too fast. The sensitivity analysis rules this out. A combined-caution variant that raises $\beta$ globally to reduce precision gain produces worse payoff, not better: $1115.0$ versus $1172.1$ for no-affect ($p = 0.003$, entropy $9.27$). Reducing precision uniformly removes the useful action confidence in the stable portions of the episode without addressing the timing problem in the betrayal portion.

Gradual betrayal confirms that the limit is the speed of change. When the partner's disposition shifts slowly, the $\beta_k$ posterior tracks the changing prediction record before a large confidence surplus can accumulate. Default precision modulation is neutral under gradual change: payoff $1147.6$ versus $1148.9$ for no-affect, $p = 0.906$. Combined caution again hurts ($1118.3$, $p = 0.005$). The affective precision channel is adaptive when partner behavior is stable and neutral when it changes slowly; it becomes maladaptive specifically when behavioral change is faster than partner beliefs can track.

\subsection{Precision-dynamics phenotypes}
\label{sec:pheno}

As a compact parameter check we verify that the affective precision channel responds to parameterization in an interpretable way. In the open graded regime a low-gain variant (small $\alpha$) produces narrow $\beta_k$ movement (mean range $0.149$ vs.\ $0.950$ for default); high-gain and cautious-prior variants produce wider movement ($1.318$ and $1.435$). The same ordering holds in betrayal ($0.132$, $1.114$, $1.091$). Payoff does not rank variants monotonically by precision-dynamics range, consistent with the regime-dependence result: the affective channel's behavioral consequence depends on the task context, not just on how much it moves.
