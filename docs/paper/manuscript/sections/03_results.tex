\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

The experiments are organized around three dissociations. The first tests whether $\beta_k$-derived policy precision tracks predictive reliability independently of realized value, and whether pooling evidence across partners destroys this relationship-specific signal. The second tests whether precision affects behavior through action commitment rather than improved partner-state inference. The third examines how the mechanism behaves under social change, and how precision gain and prior model fitness shape the temporal structure of trust revision. Each dissociation is evaluated by varying one element of the design while holding others fixed. We therefore treat payoff as a downstream diagnostic of how confidence calibration interacts with task structure, not as the primary criterion for whether the mechanism is present.

\subsection{Precision tracks predictability, not partner value}
\label{sec:model_fitness}

The affective charge formula is grounded in action surprisal, not payoff. This means the inverse-precision posterior $q(\beta_k)$ tracks behavioral consistency: reliable predictions shift mass toward lower $\beta_k$ and higher $\gamma_k$; unreliable predictions shift it the other way. The consequence is non-obvious. Once the agent has inferred a partner's type and stance, a reliably defecting partner can generate low surprisal despite poor payoff, while an erratic cooperator can generate high surprisal despite favorable outcomes. By construction, the precision signal tracks the quality of the agent's predictive model---not the desirability of the partner's behavior. The 30-seed model-fitness analysis tests whether this holds across the simulated social portfolio.

Taken raw, partner-local precision associates more strongly with action surprisal than realized payoff ($0.949$ versus $0.748$). But in partner-choice episodes, payoff, surprisal, and exposure are correlated---partners engaged more often generate more evidence on all three dimensions simultaneously. Controlling for encounter imbalance sharpens the story: the precision--surprisal association holds at $0.882$, while the precision--payoff association falls to $0.130$. The mechanism is doing what it was designed to do.

Pooling evidence across partners attenuates this. A shared $\beta$ tracker preserves the coarse ordering but compresses both associations toward weaker values ($0.380$ versus $0.049$), confirming that the partner-specific signal depends on locality, not just the update rule. The aggregate payoff readout adds a sharper point: cumulative reward ranks inversely to the mechanism's presence---no-affect achieves the highest payoff ($542.1$), followed by shared ($517.6$) and partner-local ($483.5$). Affective precision is not optimizing reward. It is calibrating action confidence. Figure~\ref{fig:model_fitness_dissociation} shows this dissociation directly.

\subsection{Shared precision tests relational specificity}
\label{sec:shared_beta}

The reliability-over-value dissociation in Section~3.1 depends on evidence remaining tied to the partner who generated it---but whether that is because of locality specifically, or simply because any accumulating precision signal would behave this way, needs ruling out. The shared-$\beta$ ablation tests this directly. It applies the same update rule to pooled evidence, keeping per-partner POMDP beliefs intact. If the signal degrades under pooling, the update rule alone is not sufficient; the relational structure of the evidence is doing the work.

It degrades. Under shared $\beta$, the surprise association falls from $0.882$ to $0.380$ and the payoff association from $0.130$ to $0.049$---both compressed, both weakened. Locality is load-bearing. Pooling evidence blurs model-fitness estimates across relationships, converting a partner-specific confidence signal into a property of the social environment as a whole. Whether that blurring costs the agent behaviorally depends on how heterogeneous the partner portfolio is---a question the mixed-volatility analyses return to directly.

\subsection{Behavioral effects arise through action sharpening}
\label{sec:deployment}

Does partner-local affective precision change behavior by sharpening how existing beliefs are deployed in action---or does it improve the beliefs themselves? These explanations are separable. A tracked-only ablation updates $q(\beta_k)$ normally but holds $\gamma_k$ fixed at $\gamma_{\mathrm{base}}$, leaving the precision estimate intact while cutting it off from action selection. If the behavioral effect survives, it comes from improved partner-state inference. If it disappears, the effect lives in the $\beta_k \rightarrow \gamma_k$ deployment step.

It disappears. In the open graded regime, tracked-only matches the no-affect baseline on both entropy ($8.79$ versus $8.79$) and payoff ($1864.2$ versus $1864.2$). Full precision modulation is the only variant that lowers entropy ($8.59$), while posting a slightly lower cumulative payoff ($1851.3$). The entropy gap is modest, but its locus is clean: the same beliefs, deployed more sharply, produce more committed behavior---and that commitment does not automatically improve reward. This rules out the interpretation that the $\beta_k$ tracker improves partner-state inference as a side effect. The beliefs are unchanged. What changes is the degree of commitment they produce. Figure~\ref{fig:deployment_dissociation} shows the three-way contrast.

That affective precision lowers entropy without a payoff gain is not a failure of the mechanism. It is consistent with viewing affective precision as a calibration layer---one that regulates how confidently the agent acts on what it knows, not one that guarantees better outcomes from acting more confidently. Whether sharper deployment translates to payoff advantage depends on what the agent is being confident about, and toward whom---a question the partner-choice condition addresses directly.

\subsection{Partner selection also sharpens under precision modulation}
\label{sec:partner_choice}

The previous section showed that action-sharpening occurs within a fixed partner interaction. Does the same effect shape \emph{which} partner the agent approaches? In partner-choice regimes, the agent must commit to a partner before committing to an action---making partner selection the upstream decision that action sharpening then operates on.

Policy entropy drops under precision modulation here too ($3.99$ versus $4.83$), confirming that the $\beta_k \rightarrow \gamma_k$ pathway reaches partner selection, not just action selection. The allocation shift is small but directionally consistent with the mechanism: the partner-local agent pulled slightly toward the cooperator ($36.6\%$ versus $34.8\%$) and away from the exploiter ($13.8\%$ versus $16.2\%$)---the partners the mechanism would assign higher and lower confidence to, respectively. Aggregate payoff does not separate at this replication scale ($393.6$ versus $393.2$), extending the dissociation from Section~\ref{sec:model_fitness}: the mechanism is not redistributing engagement toward higher-value partners---it is sharpening commitment toward more predictable ones.

Whether this redistribution produces payoff consequences depends on whether partner behavior stays stable. A one-round reallocation toward a predictable cooperator is worth little if that partner changes behavior mid-episode. The abrupt-betrayal condition tests exactly this.

\subsection{Abrupt betrayal tests confidence under change}
\label{sec:betrayal}

Accumulated confidence is useful when it matches the social environment. When a partner changes, it can become a liability---the agent enters the post-switch period with evidence that was valid under a relationship that no longer exists. The abrupt-betrayal condition tests this directly: a previously cooperative partner switches stance at round~31, and the agent must detect the change and reallocate while carrying confidence built under the old model.

The clearest result is in entropy. Under precision modulation, mean policy entropy is lower than the no-affect control ($8.36$ versus $8.74$), with a bootstrap interval that does not cross zero ($-0.62$ to $-0.14$). The action-confidence pathway is active and its effect is reliable at this scale. Joint partner-type accuracy is also higher under precision modulation ($0.372$ versus $0.266$; bootstrap interval $0.034$ to $0.185$)---the agent is tracking engaged partners more accurately while managing the post-switch reallocation, consistent with engagement being redirected rather than simply disrupted.

The payoff difference is positive but uncertain ($1185.9$ versus $1172.1$; bootstrap interval $-25.2$ to $53.2$). This is not a power failure---it is the correct result for a calibration mechanism. Whether accumulated confidence helps or hurts payoff after a switch depends on how quickly engagement can be redirected and toward which partners. At this replication scale, that variance dominates. The mechanism reshapes deployment reliably; whether that deployment pays off depends on the portfolio structure it is reallocating within. Figure~\ref{fig:betrayal_boundary} summarizes the post-switch readouts.

What this reveals is that the \emph{speed} of confidence revision is not a fixed property of the mechanism---it is controlled by precision gain and prior model fitness. Agents that revise quickly may reallocate faster but accumulate false positives toward stable partners; agents that revise slowly may sustain commitment errors longer after a switch. The profile analyses vary these parameters directly to examine how the confidence-revision process differs across agents.

\subsection{Precision dynamics as computational profiles of social trust calibration}
\label{sec:phenotypes}

Four experiments vary the parameters that govern how quickly and how strongly confidence revises: a gain sweep across eight $\alpha$ values, a factorial crossing of prior belief against $\alpha$, a forgiveness paradigm where a betraying partner reverts, and a mixed-volatility environment where partners differ in stability. Together they ask whether social trust calibration varies along a single axis---and show that it does not.

\paragraph{Precision-gain axis.}
Sweeping $\alpha$ across $\{0.05, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 4.0, 8.0\}$ confirms that gain controls the amplitude of $\beta_k$ dynamics: range increases monotonically with $\alpha$ in the betrayal regime, from $0.147$ at $\alpha = 0.05$ to $1.218$ at $\alpha = 8.0$. But payoff does not follow. Across both betrayal and open graded regimes, cumulative reward shows no monotonic relationship with gain (range $1914.6$--$1986.2$ and $1906.3$--$1987.7$ respectively). Faster confidence revision is not better confidence revision. The behavioral consequence of gain depends on what the agent is revising toward---a question that requires varying prior structure as well. Extended alpha-sweep diagnostics are in Appendix~\ref{app:extended_results}.

\paragraph{Gain-prior profiles.}
Crossing prior belief (naive versus cautious) with $\alpha$ (low versus high) defines four profiles tested in the betrayal environment (20 seeds). Each isolates a different failure mode. The naive-stubborn agent (naive prior, low $\alpha$) shows the most diffuse partner selection (Gini $0.304$) and the slowest confidence dynamics ($\beta_k$ range $0.36$)---it commits weakly everywhere and revises slowly. The avoidant-rigid agent (cautious prior, low $\alpha$) posts the lowest payoff ($1889$) despite identical gain: the cautious prior suppresses confidence even toward reliably cooperative partners, so the agent never learns to exploit predictability. The anxious-reactive agent (naive prior, high $\alpha$) accumulates confidence quickly ($\beta_k$ range $1.08$) but pays for it---it shows the highest post-betrayal commitment errors toward the switched partner (P0 selection $0.319$; high investment rate $0.121$), because the same speed that builds confidence fast also sustains it too long after a partner changes. The default agent achieves the highest payoff ($1991$), not because any single parameter is optimal, but because its gain and prior sit in a region that balances these competing costs. Quadrant figures and supporting diagnostics are in Appendix~\ref{app:extended_results}.

\paragraph{Forgiveness and trust repair.}
Forgiveness separates two things that might seem equivalent: willingness to reengage a partner who has reverted, and restoration of the confidence that partner's model once supported. They come apart. After the betrayed partner reverts to cooperative behavior at round 121, no-affect and cautious-low-$\alpha$ agents reengage most readily (selection rates $0.593$ and $0.630$)---consistent with weaker affective commitment and muted $\beta_k$ dynamics (cautious-low $\beta_k$ range $0.29$ versus default $1.01$). High-gain profiles show larger $\beta_k$ swings but not faster payoff recovery; across all variants, payoff recovery sits near baseline ($0.996$--$1.033$). The agent can return to a partner before it trusts them again. These are different things, and the mechanism tracks both independently.

\paragraph{Mixed-volatility discrimination.}
\label{sec:phenotypes_mixed}
The mixed-volatility environment poses a simultaneous test: maintain stable engagement with a stationary cooperator (P0) while updating away from an abrupt-switch partner (P2) and a gradual-drift partner (P3), all within the same episode. Discriminating between stable and shifting partners requires the confidence signal to move in opposite directions for different relationships at the same time.

Higher gain produces higher payoff in this environment (high-$\alpha$: $2054$ versus default: $1976$ versus low-$\alpha$: $1927$)---but also more false positives. The rate of unwarranted confidence reduction toward the stable P0 increases with $\alpha$ ($0.484$ versus $0.422$ versus $0.359$), and the discrimination index does not improve monotonically: low-$\alpha$ shows the strongest discrimination ($0.213$) while high-$\alpha$ goes negative ($-0.089$). Gain amplifies reactivity in both directions. In a mixed-volatility portfolio, higher gain raised mean payoff while worsening discrimination---payoff and partner-specific calibration therefore dissociated rather than improving together. Figure~\ref{fig:mixed_volatility} shows the $\beta_k$ trajectories that underlie this pattern.

Across these four experiments, gain and prior initial conditions jointly determine how an agent builds, maintains, and revises social confidence---and the dimensions they control trade off rather than co-vary. High gain accelerates both useful updating and false positives. Low gain produces stability at the cost of exploitation speed. Cautious priors protect against premature trust while suppressing confidence even where it is warranted. No single profile dominates across environments. This is why the paper treats any single profile as a computational description of a trust-calibration strategy rather than a prescription for optimal behavior. Extended profile tables, alpha-sweep diagnostics, and forgiveness/trust-repair analyses are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:extended_results}.
