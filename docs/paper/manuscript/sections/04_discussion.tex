\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Social metacognition as the paper's central object.}
The central contribution of this paper is to treat social metacognition as a distinct computational object---separate from partner-type inference and separate from theory-of-mind inference about what another agent knows or intends \cite{FrithFrith2012,Pitliya2025ToMActiveInference}. Partner-local affective precision implements this object: an estimate not of what a partner will do, but of how well the focal agent's current model of that partner justifies confident action. It evaluates the reliability of the focal agent's own partner model and is therefore metacognitive rather than mentalistic.

The three problems are complementary rather than redundant. An agent can infer partner type accurately, maintain sophisticated ToM representations, and still be poorly calibrated about when to act confidently on those inferences---particularly when a partner is reliable in some contexts and unreliable in others, or when multiple relationships must be managed concurrently. Partner-local $\beta_k$ addresses exactly this gap: action confidence tracks the recent track record of each partner model, independently of the model's representational content. This complementarity is closest to ToM active-inference accounts of social cognition \cite{Pitliya2025ToMActiveInference}, while factorized active-inference models provide a formal route for separating multiple latent components of strategic interaction \cite{RuizSerra2025Factorised}. The combination would permit an agent to infer what a partner is like, model what they know or intend, \emph{and} maintain a running estimate of how much those models currently justify decisive action---a decomposition that may be necessary for robust performance in complex multi-partner environments.

\paragraph{Individual differences and the computational basis of social trust variation.}
The profile program in Section~\ref{sec:phenotypes} does not claim that the four simulated profiles are validated clinical categories. Its contribution is more specific: it shows how two parameters, precision gain $\alpha$ and the prior over model fitness, can vary the \emph{speed}, \emph{amplitude}, and \emph{asymmetry} of social confidence revision. This is the distinction a scalar trust account misses. Two agents can have similar average trust while differing sharply in whether they update quickly after negative evidence, recover after renewed cooperation, or overreact to noise from otherwise stable partners.

This framing connects the simulations to empirical literatures on social learning, social anxiety, psychosis, and attachment without reducing those constructs to model labels \cite{Behrens2008SocialLearning,Adams2013ComputationalPsychosis,Cittern2018AttachmentActiveInference}. These paradigms matter because they separate dimensions that scalar trust models collapse: reengagement versus restored confidence, sensitivity to change versus false positives, and payoff improvement versus accurate discrimination.

\paragraph{Betrayal reveals a temporal dependency in confidence deployment.}
The betrayal result makes this temporal dependency concrete. In the 30-seed abrupt-betrayal analysis, partner-local affective precision lowered policy entropy and increased joint partner-type accuracy, while producing only a small payoff advantage whose bootstrap interval crossed zero. The confirmed readout is deployment change under abrupt social change: accumulated relationship-specific confidence reshapes how the agent acts, with benefits depending on whether the surrounding partner portfolio allows rapid reallocation away from the changed partner.

The implication is structural. A confidence mechanism built from evidence accumulation has a temporal dependency: its benefit under abrupt change depends on whether accumulated confidence can be productively redirected, whether alternative partners exist, and whether reallocation occurs faster than stale confidence can keep action attached to the changed partner. This suggests a natural extension: a volatility or change-detection channel that discounts accumulated confidence when prediction-error structure changes. This is related to the problem addressed by hierarchical volatility models \cite{Mathys2011HGF,Behrens2008SocialLearning}, but it appears here at the level of action confidence rather than learning rate.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Four design features bound the current claims. First, $\beta_k$ is maintained as an auxiliary precision tracker rather than as a hidden state in the generative model; in the full Hesp et al.\ hierarchy it is a proper inferred hidden state, allowing the agent to form prior expectations over its own future affective states and to plan around anticipated changes in partner reliability. Making $\beta_k$ a variational variable would enable richer prospective regulation of action confidence. Second, partners are parameterized environment-side policies rather than reciprocal active-inference agents. This isolates the focal mechanism, but it means the present simulations do not address the reciprocal case in which both agents update their beliefs, confidence, and policies simultaneously. Third, the profile taxonomy in Section~\ref{sec:phenotypes} is predictive rather than validated against human data: the experiments test whether different $\alpha$ and prior values produce qualitatively distinct behavioral profiles, and these simulations connect those targets to human individual differences in social trust. A stronger claim---that individual differences in human social trust calibration arise specifically from variation in an affective precision mechanism with this structure---would require fitting the model to human behavioral data and establishing that parameter estimates predict theoretically relevant outcomes. The profile predictions are offered as a scaffold for such empirical work, not as an established correspondence. Fourth, several behavioral effects should be interpreted cautiously because they are modest at the current simulation scale. The betrayal payoff advantage is small and its paired bootstrap interval crosses zero, and the profile-style experiments use 20 seeds per condition. The strongest supported claims are therefore the mechanistic dissociations that replicate across controls---precision tracking surprise more strongly than payoff, entropy reduction through the $\beta_k \rightarrow \gamma_k$ pathway, and attenuation under shared $\beta$---rather than every payoff ordering observed in the simulations.

\paragraph{Future directions.}
The most important near-term extension is empirical: fitting the affective precision model to human behavioral data from iterated trust game paradigms with individual-difference measures. The profile analyses in Section~\ref{sec:phenotypes} generate quantitative predictions about exploitation sensitivity, betrayal recovery time, trust-revision asymmetry, and mixed-volatility false positives that are measurable from behavioral time series without neuroimaging. The $\alpha$ and prior parameters could be estimated per participant and tested against personality measures, attachment style, and clinical scores to assess whether the computational profiles correspond to real psychological variation.
A second extension is ecological and reciprocal: replacing environment-side partner policies with active-inference partners that maintain their own beliefs, precision estimates, and policies, then embedding these agents in settings with communication, reputation, and coalition structure. Combining this signal with ToM-style belief inference \cite{Pitliya2025ToMActiveInference} would allow future work to study whether metacognitive action-confidence signals and mentalistic partner-state models are functionally coupled or dissociable in social decision-making.

\section{Conclusion}

Predicting what a partner will do, modeling what they know or intend, and deciding how confidently to act on one's current partner model are separable problems. Partner-local affective precision addresses the third as a relationship-specific metacognitive confidence signal. The simulations show that it tracks predictive reliability more than realized value, affects action deployment through policy precision rather than improved partner-state inference, and creates temporally structured trust-revision profiles under social change. By giving each social relationship its own model-fitness estimate, the mechanism allows action confidence to be calibrated independently across a social portfolio. Precision gain and priors over model fitness define a family of computational trust profiles for individual-difference research, positioning the framework as a mechanistically grounded account of social affect.
