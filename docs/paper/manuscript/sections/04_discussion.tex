\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

In this study, we introduced a partner-local affective precision mechanism for social active inference. Each partner model maintains its own estimate of model fitness, updated from that partner's behavioral evidence, and uses this estimate to modulate policy precision during action selection. Across experiments, the mechanism separated social prediction from social commitment: the agent could infer what a partner was likely to do while separately estimating how confidently to act on that inference.

First, partner-local precision separated reliability from value. Partner-local precision tracked action surprise more strongly than realized payoff, even when aggregate reward favored the non-affective baseline. This followed directly from the update rule: $\beta_k$ changed with the predictability of the partner's behavior, not with whether the partner was beneficial. A reliably cooperative partner and a reliably defecting partner could therefore both support high precision. In this sense, affective precision behaved as a model-fitness signal rather than a reward trace.

Second, the tracked-only ablation separated deployment from inference. In the tracked-only ablation, $\beta_k$ was updated but could not modulate policy precision, and behavior remained close to the no-affect baseline. Full partner-local precision lowered policy entropy through the $\beta_k \rightarrow \gamma_k$ pathway. The mechanism therefore changed how strongly the current partner model was expressed in action. It did not need to improve partner-state inference in order to change behavior.

Third, the shared-$\beta$ comparison showed why confidence must remain relationship-local. The shared-$\beta$ comparison preserved a coarse reliability signal but weakened the partner-specific dissociation between surprise and payoff. This matters in multi-partner settings because evidence from one relationship should not automatically change confidence in another. Partner-local $\beta_k$ keeps confidence tied to the partner whose behavior generated it. This complements active-inference accounts of theory of mind, which model what another agent knows or intends \cite{Pitliya2025ToMActiveInference}, and factorized active-inference approaches that separate latent components of strategic interaction \cite{RuizSerra2025Factorised}. The present mechanism addresses a different variable: how much confidence the focal agent should place in its current partner model when acting.

The betrayal and mixed-volatility experiments show where this confidence mechanism becomes fragile. After a previously cooperative partner changes behavior, accumulated confidence can become stale. In the abrupt-betrayal condition, partner-local precision lowered policy entropy and increased joint partner-type accuracy, while the payoff advantage remained small and uncertain. The stable result is deployment change under social volatility. Whether that deployment helps depends on the speed of change, the availability of alternative partners, and how quickly engagement can be reallocated. This points to a natural extension: a volatility or change-detection signal that discounts accumulated confidence when prediction-error structure changes, similar in spirit to hierarchical volatility models \cite{Mathys2011HGF,Behrens2008SocialLearning} but operating at the level of action confidence rather than learning rate.

The profile analyses varied precision gain $\alpha$ and prior model fitness. Low-gain profiles updated weakly and distributed engagement diffusely, whereas high-gain profiles showed larger confidence swings and stronger reactivity. In the mixed-volatility setting, stronger gain increased payoff while also increasing false-positive confidence reduction toward stable partners. Social trust calibration therefore did not reduce to a single high-versus-low trust axis. It separated into update speed, confidence amplitude, reengagement after repair, sensitivity to change, and vulnerability to false positives. These profiles connect naturally to empirical work on social learning, psychosis, and attachment \cite{Behrens2008SocialLearning,Adams2013ComputationalPsychosis,Cittern2018AttachmentActiveInference}, but the present simulations are not fitted human phenotypes. They generate predictions for future behavioral fitting.

The present implementation is deliberately minimal in several ways. Here, $\beta_k$ is updated outside the agent's generative model. Placing $\beta_k$ inside the model would allow the agent to represent expected changes in its own confidence and choose policies that anticipate changes in partner reliability, as in hierarchical active-inference treatments of affective precision \cite{Hesp2021DeeplyFeltAffect}. The partners are also fixed environment policies rather than active-inference agents, which isolates the focal mechanism but leaves reciprocal belief and confidence updating for future work. The profile analyses generate behavioral predictions rather than fitted individual-difference estimates; testing whether $\alpha$ and prior model-fitness parameters capture human trust variation will require fitting the model to behavioral time series. Finally, some effects are small at the current simulation scale, especially the betrayal payoff difference and the 20-seed profile analyses. The clearest empirical support comes from the dissociations that remain stable across controls: predictability over value, deployment over inference, and partner-local over shared confidence.

These extensions suggest two immediate next steps. The first is empirical fitting. The model produces behavioral quantities that can be measured from trust-game time series, including exploitation sensitivity, betrayal recovery, reengagement after repair, false-positive confidence reduction, and partner reallocation under mixed volatility. Estimating $\alpha$ and prior model-fitness parameters from individual participants would test whether these computational profiles correspond to stable differences in social trust calibration. The second is reciprocal multi-agent modeling: replacing environment-side partners with active-inference agents that maintain their own beliefs, policies, and precision estimates. This would allow partner-local affective precision to be studied together with theory-of-mind inference in richer multi-agent systems.

\section{Conclusion}

Predicting what a partner will do, modeling what they know or intend, and deciding how confidently to act on one's current partner model are separable problems. Partner-local affective precision addresses the third as a relationship-specific metacognitive confidence signal. The simulations show that it tracks predictive reliability more than realized value, affects action deployment through policy precision rather than improved partner-state inference, and creates temporally structured trust-revision profiles under social change. By giving each social relationship its own model-fitness estimate, the mechanism allows action confidence to be calibrated independently across a social portfolio. Precision gain and priors over model fitness define a family of computational trust profiles for individual-difference research, providing a computational account of how social affect can regulate confidence in action.
