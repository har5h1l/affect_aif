\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The results establish three dissociations: precision tracks predictive reliability over realized value, behavioral effects arise through deployment rather than improved inference, and partner-local confidence carries information that shared confidence loses. Each dissociation isolates a different aspect of the same underlying claim---that social prediction and social commitment are separable problems that an agent can solve independently for each relationship. What the simulations add beyond this design intention is the shape of the tradeoffs. Calibration is not a free lunch. Sharper deployment does not reliably improve payoff. Accumulated confidence can become a liability under change. The parameters that govern how quickly confidence revises do not co-vary along a single beneficial axis. Payoff appears here as a diagnostic of how calibration interacts with task structure, not as the primary criterion for whether the mechanism is present---because a calibration mechanism should be evaluated first on whether it calibrates, and second on whether good calibration helps in a given environment.

The reliability-over-value dissociation follows directly from the update rule, and it has a non-obvious implication worth stating plainly. A reliably defecting partner and a reliably cooperative partner are computationally equivalent from the perspective of affective precision---both are predictable, so both support high action confidence. What differs is what the agent does with that confidence, not whether it has it. This is the sense in which affect here is metacognitive rather than evaluative: it reflects how well the agent knows what is happening, not how good what is happening is. This separates the mechanism from reward tracking, from valence-based accounts of emotion~\cite{Barrett2006EmotionParadox}, and from policy selection mechanisms that weight actions by their expected payoff directly. The agent can be confident about a bad partner. It can be uncertain about a good one. Confidence and desirability are orthogonal signals, and the mechanism keeps them that way by construction.

The tracked-only ablation shows that the precision estimate only matters insofar as it reaches action selection. When $\beta_k$ was updated but $\gamma_k$ was held fixed, behavior was indistinguishable from the no-affect baseline. This rules out the possibility that the precision tracker improves partner-state inference as a side effect---the beliefs themselves are unchanged. What changes is the degree of commitment: the same inferred partner state produces sharper action under higher confidence. Affect here operates downstream of inference, on the mapping from beliefs to action rather than on the beliefs themselves.

This positions the mechanism precisely relative to existing accounts. Hesp et al.\ \cite{Hesp2021DeeplyFeltAffect} made affect endogenous to active inference by modeling it as inference over a higher-order belief representing global model fitness---the present extension makes that estimate relationship-specific, so that confidence becomes a property of individual social bonds rather than a global affective state. Theory-of-mind accounts in active inference~\cite{Pitliya2025ToMActiveInference} address what another agent knows or intends; partner-local affective precision addresses how confidently the focal agent should act on its own current model of that partner. These problems are complementary rather than competing---a richer agent would use theory-of-mind inference to improve the \emph{content} of its partner model while using partner-local affective precision to regulate the \emph{confidence} with which that model is deployed. Volatility models such as the HGF~\cite{Mathys2011HGF} regulate how quickly beliefs update when the environment changes; the present mechanism regulates how strongly current beliefs are expressed in action. The two can be connected---a change-detection layer that discounts stale confidence when prediction-error structure shifts would address the main limitation exposed by the betrayal and mixed-volatility conditions---but they are not the same operation.

The betrayal and mixed-volatility experiments reveal where the mechanism's assumptions break down. Partner-local affective precision is well-suited to stable social environments, where accumulated confidence correctly reflects a relationship that still holds. Under abrupt change, the same accumulated confidence misfires: the agent enters the post-switch period with evidence that was valid under a relationship that no longer exists. The most consistent betrayal effect is deployment change rather than payoff improvement, and payoff consequences depend on how quickly engagement can be reallocated---a quantity the mechanism does not directly control. The mixed-volatility results sharpen this: higher gain can increase payoff while worsening discrimination and generating false-positive confidence reductions toward stable partners. Reactivity cuts in both directions. Partner-local affective precision should be understood as a first calibration layer, not a complete social-control architecture.

The natural architectural complement is therefore a volatility or change-detection layer that monitors shifts in prediction-error structure and discounts stale affective confidence when a previously reliable partner begins behaving unexpectedly. This is also where the mechanism differs from standard volatility models: volatility models regulate how quickly beliefs update when the environment changes, whereas the present mechanism regulates how strongly current beliefs are expressed in action. A change-detection layer could connect the two---monitoring prediction-error structure and discounting stale affective confidence when change is detected. This would preserve the core benefit of the current mechanism---relationship-specific action confidence---while reducing the post-change liability of overcommitting to outdated partner models. In performance-oriented multi-agent systems, this would turn affective precision from a deployment modulator into part of a broader adaptive control loop: predict partner behavior, estimate confidence in that prediction, detect when confidence has become stale, and reallocate exploration or commitment accordingly.

The profile experiments use parameter combinations as computational analogues for empirically studied variation in social trust calibration~\cite{Behrens2008SocialLearning,Adams2013ComputationalPsychosis,Cittern2018AttachmentActiveInference}, not as fits to human behavioral data. Precision gain and prior model fitness jointly determine update speed, confidence amplitude, sensitivity to change, reengagement after repair, and vulnerability to false positives---and these dimensions trade off rather than co-vary. High gain can increase payoff while worsening discrimination; low gain produces stable engagement but slow recovery from betrayal; cautious priors suppress confidence even toward reliable partners. The profiles generate concrete behavioral predictions: an anxious-reactive agent (naive prior, high gain) should show rapid confidence accumulation followed by high post-betrayal commitment errors, while an avoidant-rigid agent (cautious prior, low gain) should show muted confidence dynamics and poor payoff even with cooperative partners. Whether these profiles correspond to stable individual differences in human social trust---connecting to work on social learning~\cite{Behrens2008SocialLearning}, psychosis~\cite{Adams2013ComputationalPsychosis}, and attachment~\cite{Cittern2018AttachmentActiveInference}---remains an empirical question that will require fitting the model to behavioral time series. For artificial agents, the same profiles suggest engineering tradeoffs: high-gain agents may react quickly but risk false positives and post-betrayal overcommitment; low-gain agents may remain stable but fail to exploit reliable partners quickly; cautious priors may protect against premature trust while suppressing useful confidence.

Several architectural extensions would turn the present calibration mechanism into a more complete and performance-oriented social agent. Most foundationally, $\beta_k$ is currently maintained as an auxiliary tracker outside the generative model; embedding it as a hidden state would allow the agent to form prior expectations over its own future action confidence and choose policies partly by considering how that deployment confidence may evolve~\cite{Hesp2021DeeplyFeltAffect}. A volatility or change-detection layer could discount accumulated confidence when prediction-error structure shifts, addressing the main limitation exposed by the betrayal and mixed-volatility conditions. Partner-choice policies could be made explicitly exploratory after detected change, enabling the agent to sample alternatives rather than simply sharpening or flattening action toward the current partner. Most consequentially for social complexity, environment-side partners could be replaced with reciprocal active-inference agents that maintain their own beliefs, policies, and precision estimates---allowing partner-local affective precision to interact with theory-of-mind inference in a fully reciprocal multi-agent setting.

Two empirical extensions follow from the profile results. The model produces behavioral quantities measurable from trust-game time series---exploitation sensitivity, betrayal recovery time, reengagement latency after repair, false-positive confidence reduction, and partner reallocation under mixed volatility. Fitting $\alpha$ and prior model-fitness parameters to individual participants would test whether the computational profiles correspond to stable individual differences in social trust calibration. The dissociations that are most robust at the current simulation scale---predictability over value, deployment over inference, and partner-local over shared confidence---are the natural targets for empirical validation first. Only after these dissociations are validated should future work ask whether richer versions of the architecture produce robust performance gains in more realistic social environments. The mechanism earns the right to be more ambitious by first being right about what it already claims.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Social affect has typically been described in terms of valence and arousal---the felt pull toward or away from situations and commitments. The results here support a different functional reading: affect as a running metacognitive estimate of how confidently the agent should act on its current model of each partner. Implemented as partner-local affective precision, this signal tracks predictive reliability rather than realized value, reshapes action deployment without altering the beliefs that deployment draws on, and produces temporally structured profiles of trust revision whose consequences for payoff depend on portfolio structure and reallocation dynamics.

The three dissociations---predictability over value, deployment over inference, partner-local over shared confidence---each isolate a different aspect of the same separation: knowing what a partner will do, and committing to that knowledge in action, are problems an agent can solve independently for each relationship. Precision gain and prior model fitness define a family of computational profiles along which that separation plays out differently---varying how quickly confidence builds, how far it swings under change, how readily it recovers after repair. No single profile dominates. That is the point: social trust calibration is not a dial to be turned up, but a multidimensional process whose tradeoffs depend on the social environment an agent inhabits.

The mechanism described here is a first layer. Volatility detection, adaptive confidence discounting, and reciprocal partner models would each extend its reach---but the more fundamental extension is empirical. The profiles generate predictions about behavioral time series that can be tested, and the dissociations established in simulation are the right targets to validate first. Whether partner-local affective precision, as a computational account of social metacognition, maps onto the structure of human social trust is the question the present work opens.
