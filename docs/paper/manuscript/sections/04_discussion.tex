\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Affect as social metacognition.}
The theory motivates interpreting partner-local affective precision as a form of social metacognition: the agent's estimate not of what a partner will do, but of how well its current model of that partner justifies confident action. The post-fix smoke results show that this signal changes deployment and can improve abrupt-betrayal behavior, but they do not yet establish the stronger claim that $\beta_k$ tracks partner predictability more cleanly than partner value. That model-fitness interpretation remains a confirmation target rather than a settled empirical result in the current packet.

This distinguishes the mechanism from theory-of-mind inference. ToM approaches model the agent as maintaining representations of what partners believe, know, or intend---a second-order model of another agent's mental states \cite{FrithFrith2012,Pitliya2025ToMActiveInference}. Affective precision does not do this. It tracks the focal agent's confidence in its own model of a partner's \emph{behavior}, not representations of that partner's mental states. The signal is metacognitive rather than mentalistic: it is about how well the agent's own social inference is currently performing, not about what the partner is thinking.

The two mechanisms can complement each other. An agent with sophisticated ToM inference could still be poorly calibrated about when to act confidently on those inferences---particularly if the partner is reliable in some contexts and unreliable in others. Partner-local affective precision addresses exactly this gap: it allows action confidence to track the recent track record of each partner model, independently of the model's representational content. A natural extension is to combine ToM-style belief inference \cite{RuizSerra2025Factorised} with partner-local affective precision, so that the agent both models partner intentions and maintains a running estimate of how much those models currently justify decisive action.

\paragraph{The lesion result supports a deployment claim.}
The tracked-only lesion confirms that beta tracking alone is not the behavioral mechanism: removing the $\beta_k \to \gamma_k$ link makes tracked-only runs match no-affect controls, even though the beta state is still updated. The current post-fix evidence therefore supports a narrower claim than the older bounded-error packet: partner-local precision changes policy deployment through action precision, but higher-seed runs are still needed before claiming reliable payoff gains in open regimes or a clean reward-independent model-fitness signal.

\paragraph{The betrayal result is the strongest current behavioral anchor.}
The corrected agent-choice selector reverses the pre-fix betrayal warning at smoke scale: local affect outperforms no-affect and tracked-only controls in the abrupt betrayal task, while doing so with lower policy entropy and imperfect final belief labels. This suggests that the precision channel can beneficially reorganize deployment under social volatility, but the result is still based on three seeds. The correct claim is therefore not yet a broad recovery theorem. It is a concrete confirmation target: abrupt betrayal is where the post-fix mechanism currently shows the clearest behavioral value.

The older gradual-betrayal and precision-sensitivity runs remain useful historical design context, but they were produced before the corrected selector. They should guide the next timing-gradient test rather than serve as final evidence for the current manuscript.

The implication is that an effective partner-local precision mechanism in volatile environments would need a way to modulate the weight it places on recent charge signals based on how much the partner's behavior appears to be changing. This is structurally similar to hierarchical volatility tracking in belief-updating models \cite{Mathys2011HGF,Behrens2008SocialLearning}, but applied to action confidence rather than to learning rates. Whether these two levels of volatility sensitivity---for beliefs and for action precision---operate independently or are coupled in human social cognition is an open empirical question that this work motivates.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Three design features bound the current claims. First, $\beta_k$ is an external auxiliary tracker; in the full Hesp et al.\ hierarchy it is a proper inferred hidden state, allowing the agent to have prior expectations about its own future affective states and to plan around anticipated changes in partner reliability. Making $\beta_k$ a variational variable would enable richer prospective regulation of action confidence. Second, the current implementation keeps $q(\beta_k)$ categorical rather than continuous. This preserves the inverse-beta precision convention and keeps the update identifiable in small simulations, but it discretizes the precision dynamics. Third, partners are scripted rather than themselves being active-inference agents; the mechanism is tested in isolation from the reciprocal dynamics that characterize real social interaction. The global-$\beta$ ablation---whether partner-local precision is necessary, or whether any shared precision estimate would produce comparable stable-regime gains---is therefore a core robustness check for the final rebaseline.

\paragraph{Future directions.}
The near-term priority is confirmation, not more mechanism sprawl. First, the H5 betrayal-choice result should be rerun at confirmation scale under the corrected selector. Second, the H1 reliability-versus-reward design should be rerun or revised, because the post-fix smoke no longer preserves the old surprise-over-reward diagnostic. A controlled shock-gradient study remains valuable, but it should follow the H5 confirmation unless a reviewer specifically asks for a timing-boundary analysis.

More broadly, the framework invites extension toward richer social environments. The trust game has no communication, reputation signaling, or coalition structure; all of these would introduce new sources of reliability evidence that the affective signal could in principle track. Testing with human participants would allow partner-local $\beta$ dynamics to serve as a computational model of individual differences in social confidence calibration---with faster, higher-gain channels predicted to show stronger post-cue reallocation but greater sensitivity to abrupt social change. Finally, combining this mechanism with ToM-style belief inference \cite{Pitliya2025ToMActiveInference} would allow future work to study whether metacognitive action-confidence signals and mentalistic partner-state models interact during social decision-making, and how they trade off under resource or time constraints.

\section{Conclusion}

Predicting what a partner will do and deciding how confidently to act on that prediction are separable problems. $\beta_k$ is designed as the agent's running estimate of how much epistemic warrant each partner model currently holds for guiding action---not a measure of partner value, not an improvement to inference quality, but a second-order signal about when and how decisively first-order social beliefs should be acted upon. In the current post-fix smoke, partner-local affective precision changes deployment and shows its strongest behavioral value in abrupt betrayal. The broader model-fitness claim remains to be confirmed. The paper should therefore be read as a corrected mechanism and evidence plan with one promising behavioral anchor, not yet as a completed publication-grade empirical package.
