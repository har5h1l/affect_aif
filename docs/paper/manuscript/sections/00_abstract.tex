\begin{abstract}
Active-inference accounts of affect model valence as an inference over expected action precision: affect reports how well an agent expects its action model to fit the world. Social interaction adds a factorization problem. A focal agent does not merely need a global estimate of whether the world is predictable; it must track which social partners are predictable, volatile, exploitable, or worth probing. We extend Hesp-style affective precision to a repeated multi-partner trust task by assigning each partner a local model-fitness signal. Each partner is represented by a partner-local \texttt{pymdp.Agent} over hidden partner type, partner stance, and own-action bookkeeping, while an external beta tracker updates from partner-action prediction error and modulates partner-local policy precision through $\gamma_k = \gamma_{\mathrm{base}}/\mathbb{E}[\beta_k]$. Across current-architecture simulations, partner-local precision is behaviorally active when the policy posterior has room to move, tracks prediction reliability more strongly than realized payoff, and changes policy deployment even when belief readouts remain similar. The strongest positive mechanism result is a 30-seed reliability-versus-reward confirmation in which $|\mathrm{corr}(\mathrm{precision},\mathrm{surprise})| = 0.701$ and $|\mathrm{corr}(\mathrm{precision},\mathrm{payoff})| = 0.419$, with no affect payoff advantage. The strongest boundary result is a 30-seed abrupt-betrayal confirmation in which affect lowered policy entropy but reduced whole-run payoff relative to no affect. These results support affective precision as a partner-specific model-fitness and deployment signal, not as a generic reward booster. The simulations do not validate clinical phenotypes or human behavior; they define a mechanistic testbed and a set of boundary conditions for future empirical and model-comparison work.
\end{abstract}
