\begin{abstract}
\emergencystretch=2em
Affect---the fundamental experiential substrate of emotion in psychology and neuroscience---does not merely colour inner experience. It regulates how decisively beliefs become action, mediating the transition from what an agent knows to what it commits to. The active inference framework has formalised this as a metacognitive signal: a running estimate of generative model fitness that modulates action confidence through an inferred precision parameter \cite{Hesp2021DeeplyFeltAffect}. But this formalisation has a structural gap at the multi-agent level. Emotional confidence is inherently relational---we carry it relationship by relationship, earned through each partner's specific behavioural history. We extend the affective precision mechanism to the partner level, giving each social relationship its own model-fitness estimate and independent action-confidence budget, updated from that partner's behavioural evidence alone.

Diagnostic simulations in a repeated multi-partner trust game support this mechanism while also identifying its evidential boundary conditions. Partner-local affective precision is designed to track partner \emph{predictability} rather than partner \emph{value}: a reliably hostile partner and a reliably cooperative one can both earn high precision because what they share is predictability, not valence. Current post-fix smoke results show the expected action-sharpening pathway and a corrected surprise-over-reward readout, while treating model-fitness and betrayal effects as confirmation targets rather than final publication claims. Engagement and volatility tests therefore ask when relationship-specific confidence reorganises behaviour, and when accumulated social confidence sharpens action around a model that has become stale.

The same mechanism also yields a controlled phenotype programme. Varying the precision-gain $\alpha$ and the prior over model fitness defines four computational profiles---anxious-reactive, hypervigilant, naive-stubborn, and avoidant-rigid---whose predicted signatures involve exploitation sensitivity, betrayal recovery, forgiveness speed, and mixed-volatility discrimination. These are not clinical diagnoses; they are simulation targets for connecting parameterised social confidence dynamics to empirically characterised variation in social trust and attachment. Affect, in this account, is a metacognitive signal regulating social commitment whose dynamics, failure modes, and individual variation reflect the temporal and relational structure of human social life.

\end{abstract}
