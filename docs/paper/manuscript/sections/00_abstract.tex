\begin{abstract}
\emergencystretch=2em
Social agents must not only infer what partners will do --- they must decide how confidently those inferences should drive action. In multi-agent settings this problem is local: one partner may be well-modeled and another barely understood, yet a single global confidence estimate treats both the same. We extend the affective precision mechanism of Hesp et al.\ \cite{Hesp2021DeeplyFeltAffect} to the partner level, giving each relationship its own model-fitness estimate that modulates action confidence independently. Using active inference in a repeated multi-partner trust game, we test when partner-local precision helps, when it hurts, and what determines the boundary.

Simulations show that partner-specific affective precision functions as a social metacognitive signal: it tracks predictive reliability more strongly than realized payoff, improves payoff and reduces policy entropy in graded decision regimes where the policy space is open enough for precision to matter, and shifts engagement toward more predictable partners in partner-choice settings. A tracked-only lesion---which preserves partner-belief accuracy while removing precision modulation---shows that these effects arise from action selection rather than improved belief quality. The adaptive profile is conditional: partner-local precision is useful when social regularities are stable, neutral when change is gradual, and costly when partners shift faster than first-order beliefs can track. That conditionality is itself theoretically informative---it tells us something about the temporal structure of social confidence.
\keywords{Active inference \and Affect \and Social cognition \and Multi-agent systems \and Trust}
\end{abstract}
