\begin{abstract}
\emergencystretch=2em
Social interaction requires agents to predict what others will do, but multi-agent settings add a second problem: deciding how confidently each partner model should guide action. We propose that affect can serve this role. Using active inference, we extend the affect-as-expected-precision account of Hesp et al.\ \cite{Hesp2021DeeplyFeltAffect} from a single-agent setting to repeated interactions with multiple social partners. A focal agent plays a multi-partner trust game while maintaining separate beliefs about each of four partners and a partner-specific affective precision estimate updated from prediction error in that partner's behavior. This estimate modulates the precision of policies involving that partner, making the agent more or less decisive when acting on a given partner model.

Simulations show that partner-specific affective precision functions as an action-confidence signal for social inference. It tracks predictive reliability more strongly than realized payoff, improves payoff and reduces policy entropy in graded decision regimes where the policy space is open enough for precision to matter, and shifts engagement toward more predictable partners in partner-choice settings. A tracked-only lesion---which preserves partner-belief accuracy while removing precision modulation---shows that these effects arise from action selection rather than improved belief quality. The mechanism has a volatility limit: after abrupt behavioral change by a partner, precision sharpens action around stale partner beliefs and reduces performance, while gradual change is neutral. Affect, in this account, augments social multi-agent inference by regulating how confidently inferred partner states are expressed in action---but its value depends on whether the social world changes faster than partner beliefs can recalibrate.

\end{abstract}
