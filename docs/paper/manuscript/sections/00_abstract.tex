\begin{abstract}
\emergencystretch=2em
Beyond its role in shaping subjective experience, social cognition requires agents not only to predict others, but to decide how confidently to act on those predictions. This distinction is especially important in multi-partner settings, where one partner may be familiar and reliable, another uncertain, and another predictably hostile. Affect is often described in terms of valence and arousal, but it also plays a regulatory role in this commitment problem: it shapes how strongly beliefs are expressed in action. Active inference formalizes this idea computationally by modeling affect as a metacognitive estimate of generative model fitness that modulates action confidence through inferred policy precision. Social confidence, however, is relational: it depends on each partner's behavioral history. We extend affective active inference to multi-partner social settings by giving each relationship its own model-fitness estimate and policy-precision signal updated from that partner's evidence alone.

Simulations in a multi-partner trust game show that partner-local affective precision tracks predictability rather than value: a reliably defecting and a reliably cooperative partner can both support high precision, because what they share is predictability, not positive value. In decision-open regimes, deployment changes arise through action sharpening rather than improved belief quality; in partner-choice settings, engagement reorganizes around reliability before aggregate payoff separates. Under abrupt behavioral change, precision can improve payoff even when type accuracy falls, indicating adaptive reallocation across the social portfolio rather than superior inference about the changed partner. This exposes a temporal dependency: accumulated confidence is useful when it can be redirected toward reliable alternatives quickly enough, but it remains sensitive to the structure of available relationships and the speed of change. Precision gain and priors over model fitness define computational profiles that connect parameterized confidence dynamics to empirically observed variation in social trust. Affect, in this account, is a metacognitive signal whose dynamics reflect the temporal and relational structure of social life.

\end{abstract}
