\section{Introduction}

Formal models of social cognition often bring together three problems that are useful to separate. The first is inferring what a partner is like---whether they tend to cooperate, defect, or behave unpredictably. The second is modeling what they know or intend---the theory-of-mind question of how another agent represents the world and plans action \cite{FrithFrith2012,Hampton2008Mentalizing,Pitliya2025ToMActiveInference}. The third is estimating whether one's \emph{current} partner model is reliable enough to guide confident action. Accurate type inference and sophisticated mentalizing do not by themselves solve this third problem. Knowing that someone is unreliable is not the same as knowing how cautiously to treat them; knowing that someone is predictable is not the same as knowing how strongly to commit to that prediction. Most formal accounts emphasize the content of social inference while leaving this commitment problem implicit.

In social settings, affect can be modeled as \emph{social metacognition}: an estimate of whether the focal agent's current partner model warrants confident action, rather than a direct readout of what another agent will do or a record of reward and punishment. In psychology and neuroscience, affect is often described in terms of valence and arousal---the felt pull toward or away from situations, relationships, and social commitments \cite{Barrett2006EmotionParadox}. Functionally, however, it can regulate how strongly beliefs are expressed in action: affect assigns action-relevant weight to what an agent already infers, shaping the degree of commitment rather than the content of inference. An agent that infers accurately but commits poorly wastes its social knowledge; one that commits confidently on fragile models exposes itself to exploitation.

The active inference framework \cite{Friston2010FreeEnergy,Friston2017ProcessTheory,Parr2022ActiveInference} provides a formal route from this observation to a computational mechanism. Agents minimize variational free energy to update beliefs and evaluate candidate policies by their expected free energy \cite{Friston2015EpistemicValue,DaCosta2020DiscreteSynthesis}. Action selection is precision-weighted: the same beliefs can produce decisive or tentative behavior depending on $\gamma$, the parameter controlling how sharply the agent commits to its preferred policies. Hesp et al.\ \cite{Hesp2021DeeplyFeltAffect} made affect endogenous by modeling it as active inference over a higher-order belief $\beta$ representing subjective model fitness. An affective charge signal $\phi$ drives updates to $\beta$, rising when the model performs better than a prior baseline and falling when it performs worse. On this view, affect is metacognitive rather than mentalistic: it tracks model fitness rather than partner intentions or reward history alone.

This account has not, to our knowledge, been extended to relationship-specific social metacognition in multi-partner settings. The extension matters because model-fitness estimates are inherently relational. A person can carry a well-validated model of a longtime collaborator, a fragile model of a new acquaintance, and an actively revising model of someone who has recently behaved unexpectedly---and these confidence states should not be collapsed into a single shared precision budget. We therefore give each relationship its own $\beta$ estimate and action-confidence signal, updated from that partner's behavioral evidence alone.

We test the dynamics this produces in a repeated multi-partner trust game across binary and graded decision regimes, partner-choice conditions, abrupt betrayal, and profile-style parameter perturbations. The results characterize both the functional role and the temporal boundary conditions of partner-local social metacognition. The simulations evaluate three predictions: partner-local affective precision should track model reliability more than realized payoff; its behavioral effects should arise through action confidence rather than improved partner-state beliefs; and precision gain together with prior model fitness should shape temporally structured profiles of social trust revision.
