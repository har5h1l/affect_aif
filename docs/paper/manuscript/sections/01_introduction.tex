\section{Introduction}

Social interaction is often framed as a theory-of-mind problem: agents must form expectations about what others know, intend, and are likely to do \cite{FrithFrith2012,Hampton2008Mentalizing}. In multi-agent environments, however, prediction is only part of the challenge. A focal agent may have a well-supported model of one partner, a fragile model of another, and an actively changing model of a third. Adaptive social behavior therefore requires not only accurate partner inference, but also a way to decide how strongly each inferred partner state should guide action. Being precise about a model you have good grounds for, and cautious about one you do not, is not just statistically sensible---it is behaviorally consequential. Over-committing to an uncertain model causes brittle choices; under-committing to a well-validated one wastes hard-won evidence.

This paper treats affect as a solution to that second problem. Rather than modeling affect as a reward signal or a global mood, we model it as a partner-specific estimate of social model fitness: a running signal of how well the agent's model of a given partner is supporting prediction and guiding confident action. In this view, affect augments social cognition by regulating the confidence with which inferred partner states are expressed in policy selection. A predictable adversary and an unpredictable ally may deliver similar realized rewards while presenting very different model-reliability situations; the affective signal, on this account, tracks the reliability difference rather than the reward difference.

We use active inference as the formal machinery because it naturally separates beliefs about hidden social states from the precision with which policy beliefs are expressed in action \cite{Friston2010FreeEnergy,Friston2017ProcessTheory,Parr2022ActiveInference}. Agents infer hidden states by minimizing variational free energy, evaluate candidate policies by their expected free energy \cite{Friston2015EpistemicValue,DaCosta2020DiscreteSynthesis}, and select actions through a precision-weighted policy posterior. Policy precision $\gamma$ is the parameter that controls how decisively the agent commits to its most-preferred policy: high precision concentrates action; low precision hedges across alternatives.

Hesp et al.\ \cite{Hesp2021DeeplyFeltAffect} provide a formal route from affective valence to inferred model fitness, treating affect as an estimate of how well the agent's generative model is supporting action. Rather than treating $\gamma$ as a fixed hyperparameter, they model the agent as actively inferring a higher-order belief $\beta$ over its expected action precision---the subjective fitness of its current generative model. An affective charge signal $\phi$ drives updates to $\beta$: it rises when the model performs better than a prior baseline and falls when it performs worse. This makes affect a derivative of model fitness---a signal that responds to improvements and degradations in predictive performance rather than to reward history---which is what allows it to be positive toward a reliably hostile partner while remaining informative about when to act confidently.

Our extension is to factorize this affective precision signal over social partners. A focal agent interacting with multiple partners maintains a separate belief state and a separate $\beta_k$ estimate for each partner $k$, both updated from that partner's behavioral evidence alone. This allows the agent to modulate action confidence differently across its social portfolio: more decisive toward partners its model predicts well, more cautious toward those whose behavior remains uncertain. We test this in a repeated trust game \cite{Berg1995Trust,KingCasas2005Trust} in which four partners vary in type and disposition across binary and graded decision regimes, partner-choice conditions, and betrayal scenarios. The trust game is a natural testbed because partner types are hidden and must be inferred---so there is a genuine gap between social knowledge and actionable confidence---and because varying the policy space lets us test whether the affective precision channel operates where the theory says it should.

Across all conditions, we ask one underlying question: does partner-local affective precision function as a useful action-confidence augmentation for social inference, and when does it break down? Results show that the affective precision channel tracks partner predictability rather than partner value, produces behavioral improvements in decision-open regimes specifically through action sharpening rather than better beliefs, and reorganizes partner engagement around reliability before aggregate payoff separates. The mechanism fails---predictably and informatively---when a partner changes abruptly, because affective precision sharpens action before partner beliefs have recalibrated, making the agent decisive at the wrong time.
