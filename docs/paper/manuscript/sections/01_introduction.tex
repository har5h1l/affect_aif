\section{Introduction}

Social cognition requires agents not only to predict other people, but to decide how confidently to act on those predictions. Theory-of-mind accounts and computational models of social inference address the first problem with increasing sophistication: they ask how agents form expectations about what others know, intend, and are likely to do \cite{FrithFrith2012,Hampton2008Mentalizing}. Yet accurate prediction does not by itself determine social action. Knowing that someone is unreliable is not the same as knowing how cautiously to treat them; knowing that someone is predictable is not the same as knowing how strongly to commit to that prediction. Most formal accounts emphasize the content of social inference while leaving this commitment problem implicit.

Affect offers one route into this missing dimension. In psychology and neuroscience, affect refers to the valence and arousal that give experience its motivational texture---the felt pull toward or away from situations, relationships, and social commitments \cite{Barrett2006EmotionParadox}. But affect can also be understood functionally: not merely as a descriptor of inner states, but as a regulator of how strongly beliefs are expressed in action. On this view, affect assigns action-relevant weight to what an agent already infers, shaping the degree of commitment rather than the content of inference. An agent that infers accurately but commits poorly wastes its social knowledge; one that commits confidently on fragile models exposes itself to exploitation.

The active inference framework \cite{Friston2010FreeEnergy,Friston2017ProcessTheory,Parr2022ActiveInference} provides a formal route from this observation to a computational mechanism. Agents minimize variational free energy to update beliefs and evaluate candidate policies by their expected free energy \cite{Friston2015EpistemicValue,DaCosta2020DiscreteSynthesis}. Action selection is precision-weighted: the same beliefs can produce decisive or tentative behavior depending on $\gamma$, the parameter controlling how sharply the agent commits to its preferred policies. Hesp et al.\ \cite{Hesp2021DeeplyFeltAffect} made affect endogenous by modeling it as active inference over a higher-order belief $\beta$ representing subjective model fitness. An affective charge signal $\phi$ drives updates to $\beta$, rising when the model performs better than a prior baseline and falling when it performs worse. Affect is therefore treated as a derivative of model fitness rather than reward history alone.

This affect-as-model-fitness account has not, to our knowledge, been extended to relationship-specific confidence in multi-partner social settings. The extension matters because social confidence is inherently relational. A person can carry a well-validated model of a longtime collaborator, a fragile model of a new acquaintance, and an actively revising model of someone who has recently behaved unexpectedly. These states of confidence should not be collapsed into a single shared precision budget. We therefore extend affective precision to multi-partner social settings by giving each relationship its own model-fitness estimate and action-confidence signal, updated from that partner's behavioral evidence alone.

We test the dynamics this produces in a repeated multi-partner trust game across binary and graded decision regimes, partner-choice conditions, abrupt betrayal, and phenotype-style parameter perturbations. The results characterize both the functional role and the temporal boundary conditions of relationship-specific social confidence. The paper tests three linked claims: first, partner-local affective precision tracks model reliability rather than realized payoff; second, its behavioral effects arise from action confidence rather than improved partner-state beliefs; and third, the dynamics of precision gain and prior model fitness define temporally structured profiles of social trust revision.
